# Article Rewrite Plan

## Current decision

Rewrite before publication.

The evidence pack now supports a cautious analysis article, not a fully proven accusation or finished publication version.

The rewrite should preserve the article's central political argument while reducing unsupported certainty.

## Rewrite goal

Turn the article from a broad claim into a sourced, caveated TWIS analysis.

The revised article should make three things clear:

1. Western capitalism helped create the cheap-production system the West now criticises.
2. EU policy is moving to reprice low-value e-commerce parcels.
3. Added costs may matter more for poorer consumers, but the exact consumer impact still needs direct evidence.

## Required tone

Use direct, explanatory TWIS language.

Avoid rhetorical-question framing as the main device.

Avoid moral overreach where the current evidence only supports context or risk.

Keep the argument clear for younger readers and non-specialists.

## Opening rewrite direction

The opening should still say that the West helped build the cheap China system.

But it should avoid claiming every motive or outcome as fully proven.

Preferred shape:

- Western companies spent decades moving production into lower-cost supply chains.
- Cheap goods became part of ordinary household budgeting.
- Direct platforms made that system visible to consumers.
- EU policy is now moving to reprice low-value parcels.
- The political question is who carries the cost.

## Evidence-safe claim ladder

### Safe as direct claim

- The article argues that Western capitalism helped build the cheap China production system.
- Reuters reported the EU 3 euro low-value parcel duty point.
- The European Commission reported high volumes of low-value consignments and linked this to customs reform.
- Guardian/IFS reporting supports the general cheap-goods consumer-impact logic.
- Reuters reporting supports general Shein/Temu lower-income bargain-seeking context.
- Reuters reporting supports general tariff/customs-duty pass-through context.

### Safe as cautious inference

- Added import costs may be passed on to consumers.
- Such costs may matter more to poorer households.
- Low-cost platform shopping may be part of the wider survival-shopping picture.
- The EU policy may reprice a system that poorer consumers use because other prices are already high.

### Not safe yet as firm claim

- The EU duty will definitely make poor consumers pay more.
- Shein, Temu, or AliExpress will directly pass the full cost to shoppers.
- UK poorer consumers depend on these platforms.
- Western companies deliberately created this exact political outcome.
- The current pack fully proves the historic outsourcing argument.

## Recommended article structure

### 1. Lead

State the central contradiction plainly:

The West helped build a cheap-production system, then became politically uncomfortable when consumers used it directly.

### 2. How the cheap system was built

Explain the outsourcing and low-cost production argument, but mark it as a broad political-economic pattern unless stronger historic sources are added.

### 3. Why direct platforms matter

Explain that Shein, Temu, AliExpress, and similar platforms made cheap goods visible and accessible.

Use the Reuters platform-use source only as context, not as proof of UK poor-consumer dependence.

### 4. What the EU is doing

Use the Reuters duty source for the 3 euro duty point.

Use the European Commission source for the wider policy framing: low-value consignments, customs reform, market fairness, compliance, and environmental concerns.

Keep these separate.

### 5. Why cost pass-through matters

Use the Reuters tariff-incidence source as general context that duties can become domestic business and consumer costs.

Do not state a specific EU parcel-duty pass-through rate.

### 6. Why poorer consumers matter

Use Guardian/IFS cheapflation context to explain why price rises on cheaper goods can matter more for poorer households.

Make the link cautiously: this supports the concern, not proof of the specific EU policy effect.

### 7. Clear conclusion

Conclude that the policy debate is not only about China, platforms, or customs compliance.

It is also about who pays when a cheap system that capitalism built becomes politically inconvenient.

## Phrases to use

- The evidence supports a narrower point.
- This does not prove the whole case, but it shows the risk.
- The cost does not disappear; someone carries it.
- Cheap goods are not a luxury for everyone.
- For some households, cheap buying is part of survival budgeting.
- The policy question is not only whether the system is fair. It is who pays to change it.

## Phrases to avoid

- This proves poor people will pay more.
- The EU is punishing poor consumers.
- China forced the West into this system.
- All costs will be passed on.
- Everyone using these platforms is poor.
- The evidence is complete.

## Publication path

Before publication, choose one route:

### Route A: cautious publication route

Rewrite the article using only supported and caveated claims.

Keep the article as political analysis.

Make uncertainty explicit.

### Route B: stronger evidence route

Hold publication until direct sources are added for:

- UK or EU income-group use of low-value platforms.
- platform-specific fee or price response to customs-duty changes.
- EU parcel-duty pass-through evidence.
- historic Western outsourcing and corporate-margin evidence.

## Current recommendation

Use Route A only if the article is rewritten cautiously.

Use Route B if the article keeps its stronger language.

Do not publish the current draft as fully evidenced without revision.
