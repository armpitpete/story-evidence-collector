# Final Brief

## Question

Can the TWIS article claim that Western capitalism helped build the cheap China production system and that later efforts to restrict or reprice cheap direct buying risk making poorer consumers pay more?

## Finding

The article argues that cheap direct buying from China should not be treated as an isolated consumer problem.

It argues that Western companies and investors helped build the cheap production system by chasing lower wages, lower costs, higher margins, and bigger profits.

It then argues that when the result becomes politically uncomfortable, restrictions or repricing can make poorer consumers pay more for goods they had come to rely on.

The Reuters source supports one factual component: Reuters reported that EU finance ministers agreed to a temporary 3 euro duty on low-value e-commerce parcels entering the EU from July 1, 2026.

The European Commission source supports the official EU context: around 4.6 billion low-value consignments with a value not exceeding 150 euros entered the EU market in 2024, and the Commission links this surge to customs reform, market fairness, compliance pressure, and environmental concerns.

The Guardian/IFS source supports general consumer-impact context: when cheaper goods rise faster than more expensive alternatives, lower-income households can be hit harder.

The Reuters platform-use source supports general context that Shein and Temu appeal to lower-income bargain-seeking shoppers.

The Reuters pass-through source supports general context that tariff and customs-duty costs can be borne by domestic firms and consumers, rather than absorbed entirely by foreign sellers.

## What this proves

This proves what the article draft argues.

It also proves one Reuters-reported factual point about the EU low-value parcel duty decision.

It adds official EU context for the scale of low-value consignments and the customs-reform framing.

It adds general consumer-impact context showing why price rises on cheaper goods can matter more for lower-income households.

It adds general platform-use context showing that Shein and Temu are relevant to lower-income bargain-seeking shoppers.

It adds general customs-duty pass-through context showing why import duties may become domestic business and consumer costs.

It proves that the Evidence Pack v1 structure can hold a second TWIS article proof trail with news, official EU, consumer-impact, platform-use, and pass-through context sources.

## What this does not prove

This does not prove the article's wider historic outsourcing argument.

It does not prove corporate motive.

It does not prove exact consumer price effects from the EU parcel duty.

It does not prove UK low-income dependence on direct-from-China platforms.

It does not prove the EU low-value parcel duty pass-through rate.

It does not prove measured pass-through from customs duty to consumer prices for this specific policy.

It does not prove the article is fully sourced for publication.
